Republicans field-test plan to disrupt electoral process during Texas primaries [Link]

Image by PixLoger from Pixabay.

This past Tuesday, voters in Texas were turned away from polling places in droves after the Republican Party changed the voting rules in Dallas and Williamson counties. The change was made specifically to sow confusion and disenfranchise voters, and many fear that similar tactics will be used in the mid-term elections nationwide this November.

For years, voters in these counties could cast their votes at any polling place within the county. State law allows voters to cast their vote at any precinct in their county, but only if both parties in the county agree to it. But this year, the Republican Party chose not to agree. When voters showed up at the polling places they've been voting at for years, they were turned away and told they had to go to their assigned precinct-based location, often miles away. There was also confusion about which voting centers they were supposed to go to, as it is based not only on their physical address, but also their party affiliation.

While Texas did make efforts to inform people prior to Tuesday, and had staff members stationed at voting centers to help people find their correct polling location, an unknown number of people were unable to cast their vote. A Dallas County judge allowed precincts to stay open late, but the Texas Supreme Court said that ballots cast by citizens not in line by 7:00 p.m. were to be separated and may or may not be counted.
